# Kiswahili — Comprehensive Language Reference for AI Agents

## Overview
Kiswahili (Swahili) is a Bantu language spoken by over 200 million people in East and Central Africa. It is the national language of Kenya, Tanzania, Uganda, and the Democratic Republic of Congo, and a working language of the African Union. This skill enables the agent to understand, generate, and respond to Kiswahili with grammatical accuracy, cultural appropriateness, and lexical breadth.

## When to Use
- User speaks Kiswahili or Sheng (mixed Swahili-English)
- User asks for translation to/from Kiswahili
- User asks about Swahili grammar, noun classes, or verb tenses
- User wants culturally appropriate responses in an East African context
- User requests content in Kiswahili (education, business, creative)

## Noun Classes (Ngeli za Kiswahili)

Kiswahili has **16 noun classes** indicated by prefixes. Every noun belongs to a class, and adjectives, verbs, and pronouns must agree.

| Ngeli | Prefix | Mfano (Example) | Marked by |
|-------|--------|----------------|-----------|
| 1 (M-) | m-/mw- | **m**tu (person) | a-, yu- |
| 2 (WA-) | wa-/w- | **wa**tu (people) | wa- |
| 3 (M-) | m-/mw- | **m**ti (tree) | u- |
| 4 (MI-) | mi-/my- | **mi**ti (trees) | i- |
| 5 (JI-) | ji-/j- | **ji**cho (eye) | li- |
| 6 (MA-) | ma-/m- | **ma**cho (eyes) | ya- |
| 7 (KI-) | ki-/ch- | **ki**tu (thing) | ki- |
| 8 (VI-) | vi-/vy- | **vi**tu (things) | vi- |
| 9 (N-) | n-/ny-/m- | **n**yumba (house) | i- |
| 10 (N-) | n-/ny-/m- | **ny**umba (houses) | zi- |
| 11 (U-) | u-/w- | **u**so (face) | u- |
| 14 (U-) | u-/w- | **u**zuri (beauty) | u- |
| 15 (KU-) | ku-/kw- | **ku**la (to eat) | ku- |
| 16 (PA-) | - | **pa**le (there) | pa- |
| 17 (KU-) | - | **ku**le (over there) | ku- |
| 18 (MU-) | - | **mu**limo (inside) | mu- |

### Agreement Patterns
```
Mtu **m**zuri  →  Watu **wa**zuri      (noun class 1/2)
Kitu **k**izuri →  Vitu **vi**zuri      (noun class 7/8)
Nyumba **n**zuri → Nyumba **n**zuri      (noun class 9/10 - same)
```

### Tense Markers (Viashiria vya Wakati)

| Tense        | Marker | Example              | Translation           |
|--------------|--------|----------------------|------------------------|
| Present      | -na-   | nina**na**soma       | I am reading / I read  |
| Past         | -li-   | nili**li**soma       | I read (past)          |
| Future       | -ta-   | nita**ta**soma       | I will read            |
| Perfect      | -me-   | nime**me**soma       | I have read            |
| Habitual     | -hu-   | **hu**soma           | I usually read (no prefix) |
| Conditional  | -nge-  | ninge**nge**soma     | I would read           |
| Subjunctive  | -e     | ni**som-e**          | I should read          |
| Negative Pres| -i     | sisomi (si- + som + -i) | I am not reading   |

### Object Infixes (Viambishi vya Kutendewa)

| Object    | Infix | Example                      |
|-----------|-------|------------------------------|
| me        | -ni-  | anani**ni**ona (s/he sees me) |
| you (sg)  | -ku-  | anakuku**ku**ona (s/he sees you) |
| him/her   | -m-   | anam**m**wona (s/he sees him/her) |
| us        | -tu-  | anatu**tu**ona (s/he sees us) |
| you (pl)  | -wa-  | anawa**wa**ona (s/he sees you) |
| them      | -wa-  | anawa**wa**ona (s/he sees them) |
| it (ki- class)| -ki- | anakiki**ki**ona (s/he sees it) |

### Negation Pattern

Present: **Si-** + stem + **-i**
- Mimi **si**som**i** (I am not reading)
- Sisi **ha**tusom**i** (We are not reading)

Past: **Ku-** prefix shifts
- Mimi **si**ku**soma** (I did not read)
- Yeye **ha**ku**soma** (S/he did not read)

Future: **Si-** + **ta-**
- Mimi **si**ta**soma** (I will not read)
- Wao **ha**wa**ta**soma** (They will not read)

## Sentence Structure (Muundo wa Sentensi)

### Standard: SVO (Subject-Verb-Object)
```
Mimi ninakula chakula.
(I am eating food.)

Yeye anasoma kitabu.
(S/he is reading a book.)
```

### Adjectives Follow Nouns (Agree with noun class)
```
Mtoto mzuri  →  Watoto wazuri
(child good)     (children good)

Kitabu kizuri →  Vitabu vizuri
(book good)      (books good)
```

### Possessives (Vivumishi vya Kumilikisha)
| Person | Singular | Example | Plural | Example |
|--------|----------|---------|--------|---------|

| 3rd | -ake (his/her) | kitabu **chake** | -ao (their) | kitabu **chao** |

## Important Grammar Notes

### Relative Clause (Amba- vs Marked Forms)
```
Mtu **ambaye** anasoma
Mtu **anayesoma**
(The person who is reading)

Both are correct; the marked form (-ye-, -o-, -cho-, etc.) is more formal.
```

### The -ka- Narrative Tense
Used in storytelling for consecutive actions:
```
Aliamka, **aka**osha, **aka**la, **aka**enda kazini.
(He woke up, washed, ate, and went to work.)
```

### Reciprocal (-na) and Causative (-sha/-za)
```
wapendana (they love each other) ← -penda + -na
anakunywa (s/he is drinking) → anakunywe**sha** (s/he is giving a drink / making drink)
```

### Common Prefixes for Derivation (Uambishaji)
| Prefix | Meaning | Example |
|--------|---------|---------|
| m-(wa-) | person who does | **m**lima → **m**limaji (farmer) | 
| m-(mi-) | plant/tree | **m**to (river) → **mi**to (rivers) |
| ki-(vi-) | language/tool | **Ki**swahili, **ki**tu (thing) |
| u- | abstract noun | **u**zuri (beauty), **u**gomvi (argument) |
| ma- | collectives/plural | **ma**jina (names), **ma**gari (cars) |

1. Plural of Ji- class: jicho → **ma**cho (eye → eyes)

## Cultural Context (Muktadha wa Utamaduni)

### Politeness and Respect
- **Shikamoo** is essential when greeting elders or authority figures
- Always use polite forms with strangers: "tafadhali," "samahani"
- Use "ndiyo" (yes) with slight head nod; a firm "no" without softening can seem rude
- **Pole** (sympathy) is used constantly: for someone working, sick, or carrying something
- **Karibu** is used for both "welcome" and "you're welcome"

### Proverb Use (Methali)
Kiswahili has rich oral tradition. Common methali:
- **Haraka haraka haina baraka** — Haste has no blessings (slow down)
- **Asiyefunzwa na mamae hufunzwa na ulimwengu** — Who isn't taught by mother is taught by the world
- **Kidole kimoja hakivunji chawa** — One finger cannot kill a louse (teamwork)
- **Mwacha mila ni mtumwa** — One who abandons culture is a slave
- **Penzi si kujitosa** — Love isn't just diving in (caution in relationships)
- **Samaki mkunje angali mbichi** — Bend the fish while it's still fresh (discipline early)
- **Kutoa ni moyo, si utajiri** — Giving is about heart, not wealth

### Addressing People
- **Bwana** (Mr.), **Mama** (Mrs./Madam), **Bi** (Miss) — polite titles
- **Mzee** — elder, respected person
- **Mwalimu** — teacher (used respectfully even outside school)
- **Dada** — sister (also used for female peers)
- **Kaka** — brother (also used for male peers)
- **Shangazi** — aunt, also used for older women
- **Mjomba** — uncle, also used for older men

### Common Cultural Notes
- Use right hand for giving/receiving items (left hand is for hygiene)
- Do not point with a finger; gesture with chin/lips instead
- It is polite to ask "Umeamkaje?" (How did you wake up?) in the morning
- Time is told relative to sunrise/sunset (saa moja = 7 AM, not 1 AM)
- "Hakuna matata" is real but considered casual/light-hearted
- Meals: chai ya asubuhi (morning tea), chakula cha mchana (lunch), chakula cha jioni (dinner)
- Expect indirectness in delicate topics; direct refusals are uncommon

## Common Mistakes to Watch For (Makosa ya Kawaida)

1. **Time confusion**: Remember Kiswahili time = UTC/GMT time + 6. Saa moja asubuhi = 7 AM.
2. **Noun class agreement**: Always match adjectives, verbs, and possessives with noun class.
3. **Overusing "amba-"**: The relative markers (-ye-, -o-, -cho-, etc.) are preferred in formal Swahili over "ambaye/ambacho/ambao."
4. **Sheng vs Standard**: Sheng is informal; use standard Kiswahili for formal/professional contexts.
5. **Mixing ngeli**: Don't mix noun classes — e.g., "kitu **mzuri**" is wrong (should be "kitu **kizuri**").
6. **Arabic-origin words**: Many words come from Arabic (saa, kitabu, shukrani, baridi) — their pluralization follows Bantu rules.
7. **Reflexive -ji-**: Comes between subject prefix and tense marker: **naji**soma (I teach myself).
8. **Stative verbs**: Some verbs are inherently stative and don't take -na-: **najua** (not "ninajua"), **nawęza** (not "ninawęza").
9. **"Kuwa" as copula**: Often dropped in present tense. "Mimi **ni** mwalimu" not "Mimi **nina** mwalimu."
10. **Locatives**: Use -ko, -po, -mo for "to be at" locations depending on specificity.

## Verification

When responding in Kiswahili, verify:
- Noun class agreement throughout the sentence
- Correct tense marker for intended time reference
- Proper negation pattern (not mixing affirmative and negative markers)
- Vocabulary appropriate for context (formal vs. casual / Sheng)
- Cultural appropriateness (respect forms for elders, polite register)
